On statistical reporting in biomedical journals

Apr 25, 2018 4 min read

Poor quality of statistical reporting in the biomedical literature is not uncommon. Here is another example by (Cirio et al. 2016). The study itself is well planed, executed and reported. The aim was to assess whether heated and humidified high flow gases delived through nasa cannula (HFNC) improve exercise performance in severe chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) patients. It all started when I show their Fig.1. Here is my attempt to reproduce it

x <- c(300, 320, 327, 415, 420, 428, 433, 480, 495, 548, 550, 555)*0.965
y <- (c(365, 384, 380, 480, 470, 540, 540, 550, 540, 610, 560, 999) + 12)*0.965
id <- rep(1:12)
time <- c(rep("Control test", 12), rep("NFNC test", 12))

data <- data.frame(id = c(id, id), value = c(x, y), time = time)

library("ggplot2")

ggplot(data = data, aes(x = time, y = value, group = id)) + 
   geom_line() + 
   geom_point(size = 3) + 
   ylim(0, 1000) + 
   ylab("Tlim, sec") +
   xlab("") + 
   geom_point(aes(y = (999 + 12)*0.965, x = "NFNC test"), col = "red", size = 3) + 
   geom_point(aes(y = 555*0.965, x = "Control test"), col = "red", size = 3)

In total there are n=12 patients tested twice under the control test and NFNC test. The outcome of interest is the endurance time (Tlim - y axis). This is practically how long each test lasted. It is evident from the figure Tlim increased for all subjects under the NFNC test. Moreover, this increase reached statistical significance (p-value = 0.015)! Looking at the plot I was pondered about the “outlying” patient (red dot). His/her Tlim increased by a whooping 400 seconds! This is huge compared to the other patients. Then I wondered how would the results change if we excluded him/her from the analysis? And here is where the problems start.

There is no way from the text to figure out which test was used to produce the p-value of 0.015. Is is a paired t-test or a Wilcoxon test (they mention both is the statistical analysis section)?

Having abandoned the idea of being able to reproduce the analysis I started thinking about reporting guidelines, hence the title of this post. The journal must have guidelines for reporting statistical analysis. No, it does not and unfortunately, most of the biomedical journals don’t have statistical reporting guidelines even though 40 years ago O’Fallon and colleges recommended that “Standards governing the content and format of statistical aspects should be developed to guide authors in the preparation of manuscripts” (O’Fallon et al. 1978). Since then many repeated the message. As a result, a few sporadic attempts are usually editorials such as (Cummings and Rivara 2003) and (Arifin et al. 2016).

Perhaps, the most comprehensive set of statistical reporting guidelines suitable for medical journals is given by (Lang and Altman 2013) (the SAMPL guidelines). As there are many general reporting guidelines based on the study design as such CONSORT, STROBE, PRISMA etc (see http://www.equator-network.org/) that authors in many journals need to adhere to, I believe the SAMPL guidelines is a big step forward on reporting statistics. The only journal that suggests the use of the SAMPL guidelines is the British Journal of Dermatology (Hollestein and Nijsten 2015). (I’ll keep adding the list).

Now that the guidelines exist, let’s make use of them.
